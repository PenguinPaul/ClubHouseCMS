An important aspect of nearly all video games, as you know, is artificial intelligence (AI). I personally find AI very intriguing, but as with most neat stuff it's not easy. That's something I've noticed over the years; anything that's cool is probably pretty complicated/difficult. Regardless if that theory is actually true or not, game AI has drastically improved over the years. It's been fun seeing this evolution first hand! However, game NPCs are only a subset of the overall broad CS research area of AI & machine learning. The class would serve as a nice formal intro to AI and will count towards elective credit.

I wanted to share a couple of documentaries with you concerning machine learning/AI. Both involve the technological innovating powerhouse IBM, which has a plant in Rochester, MN. The first is "Game Over: Kasparov and the Machine". This came out in 2003 and focuses on the highly publicized chess matches between chess grandmaster Garry Kasparov and IBM supercomputer Deep Blue spanning 1996-1997. It's pretty dramatic as there was a lot of controversy surrounding the second match. What makes chess difficult is that each move of foresight introduces exponentially more moves to consider. I would certainly recommend viewing the documentary and would love to talk about it more in depth once you watch it. The second film is "Smartest Machine on Earth". Here the plot focus is another IBM supercomputer named Watson, which you may have already seen on NOVA. Watson plays a different (and much harder) game than its father Deep Blue; Watson plays Jeopardy! It's the ultimate test of one's breadth of knowledge. Jeopardy is difficult because of the verbose mind-bending language of the clues. While this may be the easy part for humans, it has been a struggle for computers until recently. To be fair Watson is not connected to the Internet, rather its preloaded with information that its creators deem useful. Again, you should view this documentary even if you are only remotely interested in AI. Both documentaries present amazing feats in the realm of artificial intelligence & machine learning despite doing so in different ways. As a little bonus, I'll throw in a TED talk by the legend himself, Ken Jennings. This is to be watched only after you view "Smartest machine on Earth"; don't spoil it for yourself!
